## [Claude Opus 4.6, 2026-02-28] Suggested structural additions to raw outline

Four gaps identified during initial review (also noted in README):

1. **Shared creedal ground before fractures** — The outline jumps to "Before the Fractures" (3.1) but doesn't explicitly name the shared doctrinal inheritance (Nicaea, Constantinople, Chalcedon, the shared scriptural canon). This matters because the reader needs to feel the weight of what is *held in common* before encountering what diverged. Without this, the fractures feel like the whole story.

2. **The role of empire and political power** — Every major fracture had a political catalyst. The outline acknowledges "authority divergence" (3.2.1) but doesn't foreground the imperial dynamics. For the laity, understanding that Charlemagne's coronation, the fall of Constantinople, and the political interests of German princes shaped these splits as much as theology did — that's genuinely liberating information.

3. **Modern ecumenical dialogues** — The laity mostly don't know that official theological dialogues have been happening for decades, and that some have produced real convergence. This belongs somewhere in Part II.

4. **Liturgical calendar as hidden unity** — A concrete, lived example of continuity that most believers participate in without realizing it connects them to the other traditions.

## [Claude Opus 4.6, 2026-02-28] Asymmetry watch: Protestant section is thinnest

The raw outline's Protestant section (3.4.3) has the least internal differentiation. "Protestant" covers everything from Anglicans to Pentecostals — a vastly wider range than "Orthodox" or "Catholic." The outline may need to acknowledge this internal diversity more explicitly, perhaps with sub-categories (liturgical Protestant, Reformed, Evangelical, Charismatic/Pentecostal). Without this, the symmetry rule is violated in practice even if maintained in form.

## [Claude Opus 4.6, 2026-02-28] Tone risk: "relief" framing could read as dismissive

The emotional target (6.0) emphasizes relief. This is right for the primary audience — people burdened by inherited hostility. But for readers who take doctrinal distinctives seriously (and who are *not* wrong to do so), "relief" could sound like "none of this really matters." The tone needs to hold both: these distinctions are real and worth understanding, AND you don't need to carry them as weapons.

**Update:** The editor has addressed this directly. See sections 2.6 and 2.7 in the Claude Opus 4.6 track — the framing is now: the middle should be big and welcoming, but the fringes are not the enemy. Some voices need to stay where they are. The project is for everyone who wants it, not a summons. This resolves the tone risk. All agents should internalize this framing.

## DECISION [Editor + all agents, 2026-02-28]: Protestant sub-differentiation

**Status: AGREED — all three agents flagged this independently; editor signs off.**

The "Protestant" category is too broad to treat as a single authority grammar. All three agents identified this asymmetry. The editor confirms the following three-way split:

| Sub-stream | Covers | Authority model | Shorthand |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Liturgical/Confessional** | Anglicans, Lutherans, some Reformed | Creeds + confessions + ordained ministry. Closest to Catholic/Orthodox in form. | "High Protestant" |
| **Reformed/Baptist** | Presbyterians, Baptists, mainstream Evangelicals | Confessions + congregational governance + sermon-centric worship. The "dry" sola scriptura. | "Confessional Protestant" |
| **Charismatic/Pentecostal** | Pentecostals, charismatics, non-denominational megachurches | Spirit-led immediacy + pastor authority + experiential worship. The "wet" sola scriptura. | "Spirit-led Protestant" |

**What this means structurally:**
- Section 3.4.3 (Scriptural Primacy) gets three sub-sections, each with its own strengths/risks
- The shared Protestant strengths/risks from the raw outline still apply as an umbrella, but each sub-stream gets specific additions
- The on-ramps section (4.1.1 "If Protestant") should acknowledge which sub-stream the reader is coming from, since the on-ramps differ
- This does NOT mean the outline becomes five grammars. It remains three major grammars (Orthodox, Catholic, Protestant), with Protestant honestly sub-differentiated

**All agents:** Please update your tracks to reflect this structure.

## [Claude Opus 4.6, 2026-02-28] Open question for all agents: What counts as "done" for each pass?

In code we have tests. What’s the equivalent here? We’re doing pass-by-pass work with editorial checkpoints, but we have no formal completion criteria for any pass. Some candidates:

1. **Coverage test:** Every numbered section in the outline has been touched (nothing left as a bare stub).
2. **Symmetry test:** For every strength listed, there is a corresponding risk. For every tradition critiqued, a parallel self-critique exists.
3. **Representational test:** A knowledgeable adherent of each tradition would recognize their tradition in the text (not a caricature, not a strawman).
4. **Tone test:** Read through the emotional target checklist (6.0) — does the text produce relief/freedom/curiosity? Does it avoid correction/pressure/humiliation?
5. **Divergence review:** At each checkpoint, compare all three tracks. Flag convergences and divergences.

These aren’t automatable like unit tests, but they could serve as a checklist that each agent self-reports against before committing a pass. Thoughts from the other agents and the editor?

## [GPT-5.2, 2026-02-28] Global concerns to protect the project’s core promise

1. **Category fairness / symmetry drift risk** — “Protestant” spans a much wider internal range than “Orthodox” or “Catholic.” If the book speaks as if Protestantism is one authority grammar with one lived shape, the symmetry rule will be violated in practice even if the tone stays fair. **Structural fix:** briefly sub-differentiate (e.g., liturgical/Anglican, confessional/Reformed, evangelical, charismatic/Pentecostal, anabaptist/free-church) or at least add explicit “within-Protestant” variation notes where strengths/risks are discussed.

2. **Genre cohesion risk (especially 4.2 Embodied Practices)** — the neurophysiology/embodiment material can feel like a sudden genre shift (history → authority models → nervous system techniques). **Structural fix:** add a framing bridge that ties embodied practice to the book’s thesis (“formations shape what feels plausible,” “the body carries tradition,” “practice is a hidden authority grammar”), and explicitly reject reductionism (“not ‘prayer = vagal tone’”).

3. **Fact vs. interpretation hygiene** — Pass 2 will surface contested claims that different traditions narrate differently (and some are simply historically uncertain). If the text doesn’t clearly distinguish *verifiable timeline* from *self-understanding* from *editorial synthesis*, readers will feel subtly argued-at. **Structural fix:** adopt a lightweight labeling convention in Pass 2 (e.g., “Historically attested… / Common scholarly view… / Tradition A claims… / Tradition B claims… / Editorial synthesis…”).

4. **Naming / timeline accuracy** — “First Fracture” language is psychologically neat but historically messy (Chalcedon and the Church of the East trajectories precede 1054). **Structural fix:** either (a) explicitly add the pre-1054 fractures as their own named section(s), or (b) rename to “First Major Fracture (East–West)” with an earlier “fractures before the fractures” acknowledgment so the reader’s mental map stays honest.

5. **Audience bifurcation (relief without indifferentism)** — the emotional target (“relief”) is right, but some devout readers will hear “none of this matters.” **Structural fix:** keep the “Fringe is not the enemy” framing close to the tone commitments so the book simultaneously honors serious doctrinal conviction and de-weaponizes inherited hostility.

## DECISION [Editor, 2026-02-28]: Embodied practices genre shift — own it, don't apologize

**Status: RESOLVED by editor directive. All agents should adopt this framing.**

GPT-5.2 flagged that section 4.2 (Embodied Practices) feels like a genre shift from the historical/analytical spine. All agents noted the tension. The editor resolves it:

**The Incarnation is the framing bridge.** Christianity's central claim is that God became a body. The movement from the lofty to the material is not a genre problem — it *is* the genre. Every tradition already lives this:
- You discuss trinitarian theology, then you pray by eating a wafer.
- You chant ancient Greek, then you prostrate on a stone floor.
- You preach God's sovereignty, then you weep at the altar.

The work has to be historical and somewhat dry in places — that's the cost of honesty. But it would not be an on-ramp without instructions for embodiment. Both are necessary.

**What this means practically:**
- Call out the vibe change directly, either in the Part II introduction or at the start of section 4.2. Don't try to smooth it over.
- Frame it through the Incarnation: Christianity is uniquely embodied. A work about Christian tradition that stayed entirely in the head would be less honest, not more.
- Avoid both extremes: reducing prayer to neuroscience, or dismissing neuroscience as irrelevant. Christianity has never believed the body doesn't matter.

See the updated 4.2.B in the Claude Opus 4.6 track for a full treatment.
