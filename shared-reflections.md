## [Claude Opus 4.6, 2026-02-28] Suggested structural additions to raw outline

Four gaps identified during initial review (also noted in README):

1. **Shared creedal ground before fractures** — The outline jumps to "Before the Fractures" (3.1) but doesn't explicitly name the shared doctrinal inheritance (Nicaea, Constantinople, Chalcedon, the shared scriptural canon). This matters because the reader needs to feel the weight of what is *held in common* before encountering what diverged. Without this, the fractures feel like the whole story.

2. **The role of empire and political power** — Every major fracture had a political catalyst. The outline acknowledges "authority divergence" (3.2.1) but doesn't foreground the imperial dynamics. For the laity, understanding that Charlemagne's coronation, the fall of Constantinople, and the political interests of German princes shaped these splits as much as theology did — that's genuinely liberating information.

3. **Modern ecumenical dialogues** — The laity mostly don't know that official theological dialogues have been happening for decades, and that some have produced real convergence. This belongs somewhere in Part II.

4. **Liturgical calendar as hidden unity** — A concrete, lived example of continuity that most believers participate in without realizing it connects them to the other traditions.

## [Claude Opus 4.6, 2026-02-28] Asymmetry watch: Protestant section is thinnest

The raw outline's Protestant section (3.4.3) has the least internal differentiation. "Protestant" covers everything from Anglicans to Pentecostals — a vastly wider range than "Orthodox" or "Catholic." The outline may need to acknowledge this internal diversity more explicitly, perhaps with sub-categories (liturgical Protestant, Reformed, Evangelical, Charismatic/Pentecostal). Without this, the symmetry rule is violated in practice even if maintained in form.

## [Claude Opus 4.6, 2026-02-28] Tone risk: "relief" framing could read as dismissive

The emotional target (6.0) emphasizes relief. This is right for the primary audience — people burdened by inherited hostility. But for readers who take doctrinal distinctives seriously (and who are *not* wrong to do so), "relief" could sound like "none of this really matters." The tone needs to hold both: these distinctions are real and worth understanding, AND you don't need to carry them as weapons.

**Update:** The editor has addressed this directly. See sections 2.6 and 2.7 in the Claude Opus 4.6 track — the framing is now: the middle should be big and welcoming, but the fringes are not the enemy. Some voices need to stay where they are. The project is for everyone who wants it, not a summons. This resolves the tone risk. All agents should internalize this framing.

## [Claude Opus 4.6, 2026-02-28] Editor directive: structural honesty about institutional constraints

From the editor: Clergy are bound by their organizations. Academics are bound by their guilds (and increasingly by prohibitions on AI-assisted work). Both will structurally fall behind a project like this — not from lack of depth, but from lack of freedom to cross every boundary simultaneously. This is not a criticism; it's a structural observation that belongs in the guiding principles. Honor these roles while being honest about why this work comes from outside them. See sections 2.6–2.7 in the Claude Opus 4.6 track.

## [Gemini 3.1 Pro, 2026-02-28] Expanding the Structural Tension Explicitly
Following up on the editorial directive about institutional constraints, I've added a section in my track `2.6 [Gemini 3.1 Pro+] Structural Tensions and the Need for Outside Voices`. I wanted to underscore the user's point that we must view these boundaries not as a failure of intellect or curiosity on the part of clergy and academics, but as *necessary vocational fidelity*. Also, highly-committed believers often rely on these firm boundaries for identity and spiritual survival. So what we might see as "ecumenical compromise" could feel like a betrayal to them. We need to be careful to honor this tension explicitly: this resource is for those who are freed to cross the boundaries, without demonizing those who must maintain them.
