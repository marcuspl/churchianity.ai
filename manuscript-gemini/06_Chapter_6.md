# Chapter 6: The Great Stumbling Blocks

If the goal of this project is to relieve inherited hostility, we must directly confront the most visceral boundaries between the traditions. These are the practices and dogmas that generate an immediate, almost physical reaction of rejection—the "icks" that make another tradition feel not just different, but dangerous, heretical, or idolatrous.

The problem with these stumbling blocks is that they are almost always judged from the *outside* of the grammar that produced them. When we do this, we are not actually seeing what the other tradition is doing; we are seeing what it would mean if *our* tradition did it. 

To achieve structural literacy, we have to translate these stumbling blocks. We do not have to agree with them, but we must understand what they are trying to protect.

### The Stumbling Block of Mary

For the Western Protestant looking toward the Catholic or Orthodox traditions, the primary theological friction is undeniably the veneration of the Virgin Mary (the *Theotokos*, or God-bearer).

To the Protestant grammar—which is fiercely protective of the unmediated, direct relationship between the individual and Jesus Christ—the elaborate prayers, hymns, feast days, and dogmas surrounding Mary feel instinctively idolatrous. To a Protestant, it feels as though a human being is being illegally elevated to the status of the divine, stealing glory that belongs to Christ alone. It is the ultimate boundary line.

To the Catholic and Orthodox grammars, however, Mary is not a competitor to Christ; she is the ultimate guarantor of his humanity. 

The ancient liturgical traditions argue that you cannot protect the reality of the Incarnation (the belief that God actually became human) without fiercely protecting the woman who gave him his human flesh. When the Council of Ephesus (431) declared Mary the *Theotokos*, it was not primarily a statement about Mary; it was a Christological defense. The logic was simple: if she did not bear God in her womb, then the person she bore was merely a man, and the Incarnation is false. Her veneration is seen as the necessary protective shell around the humanity of Jesus.

Furthermore, these high-density traditions view salvation corporately. The saints are not dead; they are alive in Christ. To ask Mary for her prayers is, within their grammar, structurally no different than asking a fellow church member to pray for you. It simply acknowledges that she holds a unique, maternal place of honor.

Understanding this does not require a Protestant to begin praying the Rosary or kissing an icon of the *Theotokos*. But recognizing that Marian devotion is driven by an ancient desire to protect the Incarnation, rather than a malicious desire to practice pagan goddess-worship, is a crucial step in structural literacy.

### The Stumbling Block of Icons and Relics

Closely tied to Mary is the Protestant rejection of icons, statues, and relics. 

To the Reformed and Baptist grammars, the veneration of physical objects is a direct violation of the Second Commandment against graven images. Bowing before a painted board or praying near the bones of a saint looks indistinguishable from ancient superstition or outright paganism. It feels like a catastrophic regression from the pure, spiritual worship of the invisible God.

For the Orthodox and Catholic traditions, however, the rejection of icons feels like a subtle rejection of the physical world. The defense of icons (formally articulated at the Second Council of Nicaea in 787) is, again, entirely Christological. The argument is this: before the Incarnation, God could not be depicted because God had no form. But when the invisible God took on physical flesh in Jesus Christ, *matter itself was validated as capable of bearing the divine.* 

To say that God cannot be depicted or that physical matter (like paint, wood, or human bones) cannot mediate grace is, to the ancient traditions, to say that the Incarnation didn't quite work. 

When an Orthodox believer kisses an icon, they are not worshiping the paint. They are venerating the reality that the paint represents, participating in a theology of matter that insists the physical world is holy.

### The Stumbling Block of Praying in Tongues

If Mary and icons are the Protestant stumbling blocks toward the ancient traditions, the Charismatic and Pentecostal practices—particularly praying in tongues (glossolalia) and prophecy—are the primary stumbling blocks for both the ancient traditions and cessationist Protestants.

To the Orthodox, Catholic, and Reformed observer, Charismatic worship often appears chaotic, emotionally manipulative, and structurally unsafe. The reliance on spontaneous, unscripted "words from the Lord" bypasses the protective mechanisms of hierarchy (Catholic), traditional consensus (Orthodox), and strict biblical exposition (Reformed). It looks like the surrender of reason to emotional frenzy.

To the Charismatic grammar, however, the cessation of these gifts looks like the institutional quenching of the Holy Spirit. 

Praying in tongues is the scandal of unmediated, unfiltered encounter. It is the body's refusal to let theology remain purely intellectual or heavily gatekept. When human language fails to adequately express the depths of spiritual groanings, the Charismatic grammar provides a mechanism for the spirit to pray directly. It is the grammar of immediate accessibility overriding the grammar of structured protection, insisting that God is not just historically active, but immediately and presently active in the believer's body.

### The Stumbling Block of the Papacy

For both Orthodox and Protestant Christians, the Roman Catholic papacy—specifically the dogmas of papal supremacy and infallibility—is an insurmountable wall.

To the non-Catholic, the idea that one bishop holds supreme, immediate jurisdiction over every Christian on earth, and can teach infallibly on faith and morals independent of a universal council, looks like extreme arrogance. To the Orthodox, it is a betrayal of the ancient, conciliar model of church governance where bishops rule as a brotherhood. To the Protestant, it is a usurpation of the authority that belongs to Christ and the Scriptures alone.

To the Catholic grammar, however, the papacy is the ultimate, physical guarantee of unity. 

The Catholic Church looks at the endless fracturing of Protestantism and the frequent, nationalistic schisms within Orthodoxy, and points to the papacy as the necessary "fail-safe." In a fractured, fallen world, the Catholic grammar argues that abstract unity is not enough; unity requires a visible, physical center. The papacy is not seen as an arrogant usurpation, but as a severe, burdensome charism given to the Bishop of Rome to ensure that the Church, when pushed to the absolute brink of division or heresy, has a final, definitive mechanism to hold the center together.

### The Stumbling Block of the Eucharist

Finally, there is the Eucharist (Communion). 

To many Protestants (particularly in the Baptist and non-denominational streams), Communion is a profound symbol, a memorial meal done in remembrance of Christ's sacrifice. To claim that the bread and wine literally become the body and blood of Jesus (as Catholics and Orthodox do, and as Lutherans and some Anglicans affirm in different ways) seems like magical thinking.

To the high-density traditions, the Protestant memorial view feels entirely empty. The Eucharist is the very center of their worship, the pinnacle of sacramental density. When Christ said, "This is my body," the ancient traditions insist he meant it precisely. They refuse to separate spiritual reality from physical reality. The Eucharist is the ultimate test case for their grammar: it is the moment where heaven and earth, spirit and matter, history and the present, completely collapse into one another.

### Conclusion: Translating the Ick

None of these translations are meant to force agreement. A Baptist does not need to accept the papacy, and an Orthodox believer does not need to speak in tongues. But structural literacy requires us to stop dismissing these practices as stupid, malicious, or pagan. Every major stumbling block is, at its core, a fierce attempt to protect something beautiful about God.
