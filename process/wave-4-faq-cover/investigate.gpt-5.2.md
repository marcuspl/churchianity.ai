## Lens 1 — Consolidated FAQ (final draft) + placement

### Placement recommendation

**Recommendation: do both, but with different roles.**
- **Web**: publish as `churchianity.ai/faq` (canonical, updateable, easiest to link/share).
- **Manuscript**: include a **short “FAQ excerpt” appendix** (10–15 questions max) that points to the web FAQ for the full list and updates.

Rationale: the web FAQ can evolve as you receive real-reader pushback; the print/PDF FAQ should be stable, short, and primarily navigational (“here’s where the book addresses this”).

### FAQ — 8 categories (reader-intuitive) with concise answers

#### A) “What is this book doing?” (framing & intent)

**Q1. Is this book trying to get me to convert?**  
No. It’s trying to make other traditions legible without demanding migration. Exploration is framed as conscience-bound and optional; staying is explicitly blessed as a valid outcome. *(See: Chapter 1; Chapter 8; Chapter 11.)*

**Q2. Is this saying “all traditions are basically the same”?**  
No. The book treats disagreements as real and sometimes non-negotiable. The claim is narrower: much inherited *hostility* is driven by misrecognition, loaded labels, and vocabulary collisions, not by a careful reading of the other side. *(See: Chapter 2; Chapter 11.)*

**Q3. Why does the book keep using “grammar” language?**  
“Grammar” is shorthand for “how a tradition decides what counts as faithful,” not just “what it believes.” It’s meant to explain why people can share words like “grace” or “church” and still talk past each other. *(See: Chapter 6; Chapter 12.)*

**Q4. Does “symmetry” mean you’re pretending all arguments are equally strong?**  
No. Symmetry is a fairness constraint: describe each tradition in terms its adherents recognize, and pair critiques with honest self-critique so the book doesn’t coach contempt. It’s not a claim that all positions have equal evidence or equal plausibility. *(See: Chapter 2.)*

#### B) “What happened historically?” (macro story questions)

**Q5. Was 1054 really “the” Great Schism date?**  
It’s a symbolic marker more than a single-day rupture. Drift in language, politics, governance, and eventually violence (especially 1204) did more to make separation permanent than the 1054 exchange alone. *(See: Chapter 4.)*

**Q6. What about churches that split before Chalcedon (451)?**  
They matter—and the book highlights them because they break the simplistic “two-team” map. The Oriental Orthodox and the Church of the East are enduring, ancient Christian communities that Western narratives often forget. *(See: Chapter 3.)*

**Q7. Did early schisms happen because of “translation misunderstandings”?**  
Sometimes translation was a major accelerant—but not a magic explanation that erases genuine disagreements, politics, and institutional rivalry. The book’s point is that vocabulary failures can harden disputes that might otherwise have been negotiated. *(See: Chapter 3; Chapter 9’s Nicaea II note.)*

#### C) “Why do Christians disagree this way?” (authority + structure)

**Q8. What’s the simplest summary of the three authority grammars?**  
Orthodox: conciliar reception and continuity of worship. Catholic: a living magisterium centered in communion with Rome that can define and develop doctrine. Protestant: Scripture as final norm, with stabilizers that vary by stream (confessions, synods, bishops, associations). *(See: Chapter 6.)*

**Q9. Does the book “take a side” on the Pope?**  
It explains the Catholic claim and the Orthodox/Protestant objections, then maps consequences rather than declaring winners. The emphasis is on what each system protects and what each system risks. *(See: Chapter 4; Chapter 6.)*

**Q10. Why is the Protestant section longer than the others?**  
Because “Protestant” is not one grammar—it’s a family of internal systems. The extra space is meant to prevent caricature by naming those sub-streams and the stabilizers they use. *(See: Chapter 6.)*

#### D) “Stumbling blocks” (visceral objections and category mismatches)

**Q11. Are Catholics/Orthodox “worshiping Mary”?**  
They would say no: veneration is not worship, and Marian language is often doing Christological work (protecting claims about the Incarnation). You can still disagree; the point is to recognize what the other side believes it is guarding. *(See: Great Stumbling Blocks.)*

**Q12. Are icons just “idols with better PR”?**  
In icon theology, the honor shown to an image passes to the person depicted; worship is reserved for God alone. The real dispute is whether the Incarnation changes what “no graven images” means in practice—serious Christians land on both sides. *(See: Chapter 9; Great Stumbling Blocks.)*

**Q13. Is theosis just “works righteousness”?**  
The book frames this as a category mismatch: therapeutic/healing anthropology vs forensic/legal anthropology. In the therapeutic frame, effort is participation in healing, not currency to purchase a verdict. *(See: Great Stumbling Blocks.)*

**Q14. Is the Eucharist “symbol vs real presence,” and is the book mocking Protestants?**  
The book tries to translate how each side hears the other from the outside, then widens the range: “Protestant” includes memorial emphases *and* strong presence claims (Lutheran, Anglican, some Reformed). The goal is accurate mapping, not ranking sincerity. *(See: Great Stumbling Blocks.)*

**Q15. Is *sola scriptura* self-contradictory because “the Church made the Bible”?**  
The book treats this as a real objection and then describes the tradeoff: centralized authority concentrates risk; distributed authority fragments. Different traditions choose different failure modes because they fear different disasters. *(See: Great Stumbling Blocks; Chapter 6.)*

#### E) Safety, trauma, and coercion

**Q16. My church hurt me. I’m not looking for “relief”—I’m looking for accountability.**  
“Relief” in this project is aimed at inherited theological hostility, not at minimizing abuse. The book names institutional failure modes, but it is not a substitute for therapy, justice, or lament; if you need those first, the book will wait. *(See: Chapter 6; Chapter 9.)*

**Q17. Does the book assume it’s safe for everyone to visit other churches?**  
No. It encourages curiosity while distinguishing disorientation from danger. Exploration is optional and should be bounded by your safety needs and conscience; leaving coercive environments is explicitly permitted. *(See: Chapter 8; Chapter 9.)*

#### F) Method and AI (skeptic questions that must be answered cleanly)

**Q18. Can AI write theology? Doesn’t theology require prayer and a soul?**  
AI can’t pray, repent, receive sacraments, or acquire *phronema*. The claim isn’t “AI replaces the Church,” but “AI can help map contested territory,” with humans responsible for governance, citations, and final editorial judgment. *(See: Note on Authorship; Chapter 13.)*

**Q19. If AI is trained on human data, isn’t it biased?**  
Yes. Systems tend to overweight Western, English, and digitized sources, and they can inherit bias from the questions asked. The book’s safeguards are: multiple models, symmetry rules, citations, and human editorial responsibility—none of which equal perfect neutrality. *(See: Note on Authorship; Chapter 13.)*

**Q20. Why three models?**  
Triangulation. Independent drafts expose stable territory (where different systems converge) and highlight disagreement zones where the book should lower confidence and increase citation discipline. *(See: Note on Authorship; Chapter 13.)*

#### G) “But what about…?” (scope limitations + honest boundary-setting)

**Q21. Why didn’t you map today’s splits over sexuality, gender, and politics?**  
Scope. The book maps historic macro-fractures and the underlying authority grammars that also shape modern controversies. A fair map of live disputes would require a different wave (and likely a different book) to avoid turning into culture-war content. *(See: Chapter 6A; Chapter 13 for method limits.)*

**Q22. What about Vatican II?**  
It’s under-treated relative to its importance for Catholic liturgy, ecumenical posture, and modern Catholic self-understanding. The FAQ should name that omission directly rather than pretending it’s covered. *(Manuscript gap; see “Gaps/deferred list” below.)*

**Q23. What about the essence–energies distinction (Palamism)?**  
It’s a major East–West theological difference and currently doesn’t get a clean, dedicated paragraph. Theosis/apophatic themes are present, but readers will still ask for the explicit frame. *(Manuscript gap; see below.)*

**Q24. What about the Oriental Orthodox “vanishing” after Chapter 3?**  
Real limitation. The three-grammar (Orthodox/Catholic/Protestant) frame is Western-centric and doesn’t accommodate these ancient churches well; the honest move is to name that limitation, not justify it. *(Scope limitation; see below.)*

**Q25. What about Anglicanism as a “via media”?**  
Anglican self-understanding doesn’t fit neatly into a single bucket, and collapsing it into “Protestant” can feel inaccurate. It’s better described as a mixed grammar with internal diversity. *(Partial coverage; see below.)*

**Q26. What about the Global South / non-Western Christianity?**  
This is the book’s most important structural limitation. A global map would need much more space for African Initiated Churches, Asian contexts, and Latin American Pentecostalism; the three-grammar frame captures some realities but not enough. *(Scope limitation; see below.)*

**Q27. What about colonialism / Christianity and empire in later centuries?**  
The book treats early empire entanglements and the Crusades, but does not systematically address colonial modernity and missionary imperialism. That absence should be named plainly, especially for readers from colonized communities. *(Scope limitation; see below.)*

#### H) Practical “what do I do now?” (reader action without coercion)

**Q28. If I want to explore another tradition, what’s a safe first step?**  
Start with observation, not evaluation. Don’t take communion where it violates conscience or discipline; ask simple questions; keep a clear “I’m here to learn” posture. If a community can’t tolerate respectful curiosity, that’s information too. *(See: Chapter 8.)*

**Q29. What should I do if the “surrender” language is triggering for me?**  
Treat safety as non-negotiable: shorten practices, keep your eyes open, stop when needed, and avoid communities that interpret distress as “lack of faith.” Spiritual practices are not a mandate to endure coercion. *(See: Chapter 9.)*

**Q30. If I don’t want to explore, is this still for me?**  
Yes. You can use the map to reduce contempt and lower the temperature in conversations without crossing a boundary. “Staying” is not framed as cowardice; it’s framed as a legitimate, conscience-bound choice. *(See: Chapter 8; Chapter 11.)*

## Lens 2 — Book cover concept brief (designer-ready)

### Quick comparable-pattern notes (what to emulate / avoid)

Two common failure modes in adjacent spaces:
- **Too academic**: heavy serif + classical painting + textbook vibe (signals “seminary,” not “churchgoing lay reader”).  
- **Too “Christian living”**: stock landscape + cheerful sans + devotional vibe (signals “inspirational paperback,” not serious nonfiction).

What this book needs to communicate, at thumbnail size:
- **Serious nonfiction** (trust, gravity)
- **Accessible** (not academic gatekeeping)
- **Ancient + computational** (the method is explicit, not hidden)

Working metadata for designer:
- **Title:** *Churchianity.ai — Three AIs Walk Into a Schism*  
- **Subtitle:** *An Experiment in Computational Ecclesiology*  
- **Author line (placeholder):** Marcus (or pen name as chosen)  
- **Website (back cover):** `churchianity.ai`

### Concept 1 — “The Algorithmic Tapestry” (heritage + computation, not sci‑fi)

**Mood:** ancient and luminous, with quiet mathematical precision.  
**Visual idea:** a mosaic / stained-glass texture (abstracted; not a specific copyrighted image) with a subtle overlay of a node‑and‑edge graph or geometric mesh. “Glitch” cues should be restrained (more “data overlay” than cyberpunk).  
**Typography:** title in a clean modern sans or subtle monospaced; subtitle in a classic serif. Large title for thumbnail legibility.  
**Palette:** Byzantine gold + deep lapis / charcoal; warm and credible, not neon.  
**Why it fits:** communicates “church history” and “computation” simultaneously without making the book look like a tech manifesto.

### Concept 2 — “Three Paths / Fracture Map” (minimalist serious nonfiction)

**Mood:** high-end nonfiction; restrained, confident.  
**Visual idea:** three lines (or three braided strands) that begin unified, then diverge, then run in parallel—hinting at both “schism” and “mapping.” Optionally: subtle coordinate grid / contour lines, like a map.  
**Typography:** bold, modern sans; subtitle smaller, widely tracked; generous whitespace.  
**Palette:** off‑white/cream background; three muted inks (oxblood, slate, ochre) with one accent.  
**Why it fits:** instantly legible at thumbnail size; avoids kitsch; still signals “structure.”

### Concept 3 — “Council Minutes / Code Diff” (text-forward, method-forward)

**Mood:** documentary + computational: “we ran a structured experiment.”  
**Visual idea:** a page-like field with layered typographic textures: faint council-like marginalia blocks + a subtle “diff” motif (add/remove marks) or bracket/brace glyphs, kept abstract enough to avoid “programmer joke.”  
**Typography:** strong serif for the “bookish” signal, paired with a small monospaced accent used only for `.ai` or a single line.  
**Palette:** parchment / near-black / one accent (deep blue or deep red).  
**Why it fits:** foregrounds the method without going futuristic; reads as serious.

### Commissioning recommendation (budget-aware)

Given a **$200–500** budget, prioritize “legit book-cover competence” over novelty.

- **99designs contest**: good for seeing multiple directions quickly; Bronze/Silver tier aligns with budget. Ensure you specify: “print wrap PDF + ebook cover + fonts licensed + stock licenses documented.” *(See: `https://99designs.com/book-cover-design`.)*
- **Fiverr Pro**: potentially within budget; insist on portfolio proof and explicit licensing for any stock elements.  
- **Reedsy marketplace**: typically higher than budget, but highest probability of “real publishing-level” results. *(See: `https://reedsy.com/design/book-cover-design`.)*

Non-negotiables in the brief:
- no unlicensed stock, no AI-generated copyrighted lookalikes, no “Matrix code” clichés
- deliver editable source files + print-ready PDF + ebook cover image
- design must remain readable as a small thumbnail

### Print spec checklist (KDP + IngramSpark compatible)

Use **templates** once final page count/paper choice is known:
- KDP cover calculator/template generator: `https://kdp.amazon.com/cover-calculator`

Minimums / requirements to tell the designer up front:
- **Trim size (recommended):** 6" × 9" trade paperback  
- **Cover file:** single **print-ready PDF** wrap (back + spine + front) *(KDP requirement: `https://kdp.amazon.com/en_US/help/topic/G201953020`.)*  
- **Bleed:** add **0.125"** to top/bottom/outside edges (KDP)  
- **Spine text minimum:** KDP requires **79+ pages** for spine text  
- **Color space:** **CMYK** for print; avoid spot colors  
- **Resolution:** images **300 DPI** minimum (print)  
- **Barcode:** leave back-cover barcode area clear (or omit if platform adds it)  
- **Safe margins:** keep critical text well inside trim; avoid thin borders (print variance makes borders look bad)

Spine width (KDP black-and-white formulas; depends on paper choice):
- **Cream:** spine width \(=\) page_count × **0.0025"**  
- **White:** spine width \(=\) page_count × **0.002252"**  

Example: ~200 pages  
- cream: 200 × 0.0025" = **0.50"**  
- white: 200 × 0.002252" ≈ **0.45"**

Deliverables to request from designer:
- print wrap PDF (KDP + IngramSpark ready)
- separate **front cover** image (ebook / marketing)
- editable source file (AI/PSD/INDD) + outlined-font export
- a “thumbnail check” PNG/JPG
