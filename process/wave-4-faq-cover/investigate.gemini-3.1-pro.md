### The Consolidated FAQ

#### A. "What is this book doing?" (Framing & Intent)

**Q1. Is this book trying to get me to convert?**
No. It is trying to make other traditions legible to you without demanding that you migrate. If you decide to explore another tradition, the book frames that as an optional matter of conscience. If you stay where you are, the book explicitly blesses staying.

**Q2. Is this just saying "all traditions are exactly the same"?**
No. The book repeatedly insists that the disagreements are real and substantive. The claim is that much of the *hostility* between traditions is driven by misrecognition and vocabulary collisions, not that all doctrines secretly converge.

**Q3. Why does the book keep talking about "authority grammars"?**
"Grammar" is the book’s metaphor for describing *how* a tradition decides what counts as faithful, not just *what* it currently believes. A grammar is a rule-set for making sense. It explains why people can use the exact same vocabulary (like "grace" or "church") but talk completely past each other. 

**Q4. Does the "symmetry" rule mean you're pretending all arguments are equally good?**
No. Symmetry means each tradition is described in a way its own adherents would recognize, and every tradition's critique of another is paired with an honest self-critique. It is a fairness constraint to prevent the author from coaching contempt; it is not an epistemic claim that all positions have equal historical or theological evidence.

#### B. "What happened historically?"

**Q5. Was 1054 really "the" Great Schism?**
1054 is a symbolic marker more than a single-day rupture. The book argues that slow linguistic drift, political rivalry, and later violence (especially the tragic sack of Constantinople in 1204) did much more to make the separation permanent than the mutual excommunications of 1054.

**Q6. What about the churches that split before Chalcedon (451)?**
The Oriental Orthodox (Coptic, Armenian, Syriac, Ethiopian) and the Church of the East are older, enduring fractures that are often invisible in Western history. The book highlights them to break the simplistic "two-team" mental model. Crucially, modern ecumenical dialogues suggest that these earliest schisms were largely driven by a failure of linguistic translation, a pattern that repeats throughout history.

#### C. "Why do Christians disagree this way?"

**Q7. What is the simplest summary of the three authority grammars?**
*Orthodox:* Conciliar reception and liturgical continuity. 
*Catholic:* A living Magisterium centered in communion with Rome that guides doctrinal development.
*Protestant:* Scripture as the final norm, with stabilizing mechanisms (like confessions or synods) that vary by sub-stream. 

**Q8. Does the book take a position on the Pope?**
It describes the Catholic claim (magisterial development and Vatican I) and the Orthodox/Protestant objection (the first-millennium conciliar pattern). Rather than adjudicating final truth, it maps the structural consequences: centralization produces incredible unity but concentrates the risk of abuse; decentralization prevents tyranny but guarantees fragmentation.

**Q9. Why is the Protestant section so much longer than the others?**
Because "Protestant" is not a single grammar; it spans multiple internal operating systems (Liturgical, Confessional, and Spirit-led). The larger footprint in the book is itself a structural observation about the reality of post-Reformation fragmentation and growth.

#### D. "Stumbling Blocks" (Practice & Visceral Reactions)

**Q10. Are Catholics and Orthodox worshiping Mary and kissing idols?**
They would say absolutely not. In their grammar, veneration (*proskynesis*) passes through the image to the person depicted, while worship (*latreia*) is strictly reserved for God alone. Furthermore, Marian titles (like *Theotokos*, Mother of God) function as ancient theological guardrails designed specifically to protect the truth of the Incarnation. 

**Q11. Is the Orthodox concept of *theosis* just "works righteousness"?**
The book frames this as a category mismatch. The Western (Protestant/Catholic) view of salvation is largely *forensic* and legal—a courtroom verdict. The Eastern view is *therapeutic* and medical—a hospital healing. In the Eastern framework, human effort (fasting, prayer) is not a currency used to buy a verdict; it is participation in the healing process.

**Q12. Is *sola scriptura* self-contradictory if the early Church compiled the Bible?**
The book presents this as a real outside critique that Protestants must grapple with. It then frames the historical reality: the choice is not between a perfect system and a flawed one. It is a choice between centralized authority (which concentrates risk) and distributed authority (which fragments).

#### E. Safety, Trauma, and Coercion

**Q13. My tradition hurt me. I'm not interested in "relief" — I need accountability.**
The book names institutional failures plainly—the sex abuse crisis, the SBC scandal, spiritual abuse in charismatic communities. The emotional target of "relief" is meant for inherited theological hostility, not for victims of abuse. This book is a map, not a substitute for lament, therapy, or justice. 

**Q14. Does this book assume it is safe for everyone to visit other churches?**
No. It encourages curiosity but explicitly distinguishes between feeling *disoriented* and feeling *unsafe*. It frames exploration as entirely optional and strictly bound by your own conscience and safety requirements. 

#### F. Method and AI

**Q15. Why should I trust theology written by an AI?**
You shouldn’t trust it the same way you trust a saint or a pastor. AI cannot pray, it has no soul, and it has no lived experience of God. The book claims a narrower advantage: AI has no tribal defensiveness. It is "positionally free" to map contested territory without getting triggered. The map is useful; but the map is not the territory.

**Q16. If AI is trained on human data, isn't it biased?**
Yes. Large Language Models are statistically weighted toward Western, English-language, easily digitized sources. We attempted to counter this by having three different models audit each other, but the book likely still carries a Western structural bias.

#### G. "But What About..." (Scope Limitations)

*Note: The following questions reveal genuine scope limitations. They are answered honestly here, rather than pretending the book covers them.*

**Q17. What about modern splits over sexuality, gender, and politics?**
The book deliberately limits its scope to the historical macro-fractures (1054, 1517). While the "authority grammars" outlined here absolutely drive how modern culture-war splits play out, we chose to map the ancient fault lines first. If you understand how a tradition views authority, you will understand how they approach modern controversies. 

**Q18. What about the Global South? Christianity's center of gravity has shifted.**
This is the book's most significant limitation. African Initiated Churches, Chinese house churches, and Latin American Pentecostalism do not always fit neatly into the Western Orthodox/Catholic/Protestant framework. A complete map of global Christianity would require another book entirely.

## Lens 2: Book Cover — Concept and Direction

**Context & Comparable Titles:**
In the "theology for laity" and church history spaces, covers tend to fall into two traps:
1.  **Too Academic:** Heavy serif fonts, public-domain classical paintings, looks like a seminary textbook (e.g., Jaroslav Pelikan's works).
2.  **Too "Christian Living":** Cheerful sans-serif fonts, stock photos of mountains or coffee cups, looks like a 6-week devotional (e.g., standard evangelical paperbacks).

Because this book bridges church history *and* artificial intelligence, the cover needs to signal **both antiquity and computation**, without leaning into sci-fi cyberpunk tropes (no glowing blue brains or matrix code).

### Concept 1: "The Algorithmic Tapestry" (Editor's Choice)
*   **Mood:** Ancient but mathematically precise.
*   **Imagery:** Take a classic piece of Christian art (e.g., a Byzantine mosaic of a council, or a stained glass window) and subtly "glitch" or pixelate specific tiles, or overlay a faint, elegant geometric mesh/graph node structure over it. 
*   **Typography:** The title "Churchianity.ai" in a clean, modern, slightly monospaced font (signaling code/tech), with the subtitle in a classic, elegant serif (signaling history/theology). 
*   **Color Palette:** Deep, rich theological colors—Byzantine gold, lapis lazuli blue, or deep crimson, contrasted with stark white or minimalist black for the typography.

### Concept 2: "The Three Paths" (Minimalist)
*   **Mood:** High-end non-fiction, Malcolm Gladwell / O'Reilly Media vibe.
*   **Imagery:** No faces or historical art. Instead, use a stark, minimalist visual metaphor of fracture and mapping. For example: three distinct lines (representing the three grammars/AIs) moving across the cover. They start braided together at the bottom, fracture in the middle, and run parallel at the top. 
*   **Typography:** Bold, massive sans-serif for the title, dominating the top half of the cover. 
*   **Color Palette:** Stark. Cream/off-white background with the three lines in distinct, muted colors (e.g., oxblood red, slate grey, ochre). 

### Print Requirements (For the Designer Brief)
When commissioning, provide these exact specs to the designer so the files are KDP/IngramSpark ready:
*   **Format:** Full wraparound cover (Front, Spine, Back) delivered as a single print-ready PDF.
*   **Dimensions:** 6" x 9" (standard trade paperback).
*   **Bleed:** Must include an additional 0.125" (3.2mm) bleed on all outer edges.
*   **Spine Width:** Assuming ~200 pages on standard 55lb cream paper, the spine will be approximately **0.45 inches** (11.4mm). *(Note: The designer will need the exact final page count to generate the final template).*
*   **CMYK Color Space:** Essential for print. 
*   **Barcode Area:** Leave a blank 2" x 1.2" rectangle in the bottom right corner of the back cover for the ISBN barcode.

### Commissioning Recommendations
*   **Reedsy Marketplace:** Best quality, but highest price ($400-$800). You are hiring vetted traditional publishing professionals. Highly recommended if budget allows. 
*   **99designs:** Good for seeing multiple concepts ($300-$500). You run a contest and choose the winner. Good if you want to see the "Algorithmic Tapestry" and "Minimalist" ideas executed before committing. 
*   **Fiverr Pro:** Best budget option ($150-$300). Only use "Pro" verified sellers for book covers, otherwise you risk getting AI-generated slop or stolen stock assets that will fail KDP's copyright checks.
