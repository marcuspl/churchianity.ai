## 03_Chapter_3.md

### 10. Alexandrian and Antiochene shorthand is reductive

- **Location:** "The five great centers — Rome, Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, and Jerusalem — each had their own character..."
- **Lens:** Polish / Integrity
- **Finding:** Alexandria as "speculative and allegorical" is fair but reductive (also deeply liturgical and sacramental). Antioch as "literal and exegetical" compresses complexity. The shorthand works for navigation but may mislead.
- **Suggested fix:** Add a brief qualifier: "in their theological emphasis" or "in scholarly method" to signal the simplification.

### 11. Canon formation presented as more settled than it is

- **Location:** "Scripture itself was embedded in worship. The biblical canon was received and stabilized in large part through what was read aloud in the liturgy..."
- **Lens:** Integrity
- **Finding:** Reasonable scholarly position (Brevard Childs), but not universal consensus. Presented without qualification as established fact.
- **Suggested fix:** Soften: "The biblical canon is believed by many scholars to have been shaped significantly by liturgical usage..." or cite scholars.

### 12. Eucharistic agreement overstated

- **Location:** "The Eucharist — the shared meal at the center of Christian worship — was practiced universally from the earliest period, and its importance was not a matter of debate."
- **Lens:** Integrity
- **Finding:** The meal was universal, but its *theology* was debated from the start (Corinthian divisions, Justin Martyr, East-West disputes about presence). "Not a matter of debate" risks misleading.
- **Suggested fix:** "The practice was universal and its importance unquestioned; its theology was debated from the start."

### 13. "Functionally agreed upon" needs refinement

- **Location:** "The core New Testament canon was functionally agreed upon by the late fourth century."
- **Lens:** Integrity
- **Finding:** Agreed upon at the Greek-speaking level (Athanasius's Festal Letter) and later in the West (Carthage 397). The Eastern canon was not formally settled until later. "Functionally" does some work but may mislead about the degree of agreement.
- **Suggested fix:** "The core New Testament canon gained acceptance in the Greek-speaking and Latin-speaking worlds by the late fourth century, though formal ecumenical recognition came later."

### 14. Chalcedonian convergence claim slightly oversold

- **Location:** "Modern ecumenical dialogues in the late twentieth century have largely confirmed this..."
- **Lens:** Integrity
- **Finding:** The Christological agreement is real but not universally accepted. Some Oriental Orthodox scholars remain cautious; some Eastern Orthodox traditionalists reject the conclusion.
- **Suggested fix:** Soften: "Modern ecumenical dialogues have suggested substantial convergence..."

### 15. Constantine's concern reduced to administrative

- **Location:** "Constantine's concern was not primarily metaphysical. It was administrative: a divided church meant a divided empire"
- **Lens:** Integrity / Balance
- **Finding:** Fair but incomplete. Constantine did care about metaphysics; reducing his concern to administrative control risks caricaturing in the opposite direction.
- **Suggested fix:** "Constantine's concerns were interwoven: administrative (unity), theological (orthodoxy), and spiritual (faith)."

## 04_Chapter_4.md

### 16. East framed as static, West as dynamic

- **Location:** "Latin theology developed its own vocabulary... while Greek theology continued in the framework of the Cappadocians"
- **Lens:** Integrity
- **Finding:** "Continued" vs. "developed" subtly frames the East as static and the West as dynamic. Both traditions developed and adapted.
- **Suggested fix:** "Latin theology developed new vocabulary..." and "Greek theology, building on the Cappadocians, continued to develop..."

### 17. Immaculate Conception genealogy oversimplified

- **Location:** "The Western formulation drove centuries of anxiety about unbaptized infants and fueled the development of the doctrine of the Immaculate Conception."
- **Lens:** Integrity
- **Finding:** The Marian doctrine had multiple sources (liturgical devotion, Augustine, Scotism, etc.). Original sin was one factor, not the sole driver.
- **Suggested fix:** "The Western formulation, combined with Marian piety and theological development, contributed to the doctrine of the Immaculate Conception."

### 18. Editorial notes disrupt narrative flow

- **Location:** "The three-layer model is an analytical tool developed during this project..." (Editorial Note blockquote)
- **Lens:** Flow
- **Finding:** Helpful and transparent, but appears mid-chapter in a blockquote. Readers may not realize it's scaffolding rather than content. Placement is disruptive.
- **Suggested fix:** Move editorial notes to a consistent location (end of chapter, or appendix).

### 19. Orthodox position framed more negatively than Catholic on development

- **Location:** "The Catholic answer... is that doctrine develops organically under the guidance of the Holy Spirit... The Orthodox answer is that the faith was delivered once and for all... no subsequent development can add to what was received."
- **Lens:** Integrity / Symmetry
- **Finding:** Catholic answer emphasizes growth and guidance; Orthodox answer emphasizes limits and closure. Reframe to show both as positive claims.
- **Suggested fix:** "The Orthodox answer is that the faith was delivered once, and that ecumenical councils articulate it definitively for each generation, preserving rather than adding."

## 05_Chapter_5.md

### 20. "Self-harm" carries modern connotations

- **Location:** "fasting to the point of self-harm"
- **Lens:** Polish
- **Finding:** Historically attested, but "self-harm" carries modern connotations (eating disorders, self-injury) that psychologize medieval asceticism.
- **Suggested fix:** Reframe in Luther's own terms: "fasting to the point of weakening his health."

### 21. Indulgence-financing claim slightly overstated

- **Location:** "was directly financing the construction of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome"
- **Lens:** Integrity
- **Finding:** The connection is real but the degree and directness are debated. Some money went to other projects.
- **Suggested fix:** "was a significant source of financing for the construction of St. Peter's Basilica."

### 22. "For the first time" overshoots historically

- **Location:** "For the first time, lay Christians could read Paul's letter to the Romans without a priest's mediation."
- **Lens:** Integrity
- **Finding:** Lay Christians could own Latin Bibles in the medieval period; the Waldensians had vernacular translations. What the printing press made possible was *mass* availability.
- **Suggested fix:** "For the first time at scale, ordinary lay Christians could own and read Scripture without institutional mediation."

### 23. Sola scriptura risk named without inverse for centralization

- **Location:** "The pattern is structural: *sola scriptura* without a shared interpretive authority produces multiplication."
- **Lens:** Integrity / Symmetry
- **Finding:** Fair observation, but the book should acknowledge that centralized authority also produces the same pattern of reform-generating-new-problems. The symmetry rule requires naming the inverse.
- **Suggested fix:** Add a sentence acknowledging that centralized models face the inverse problem: reforms come slowly and produce backlash when they finally arrive.
