# Chapter 6a: Stumbling Blocks (The “Ick” → The “Protection” Translation)

Some disagreements are not only intellectual. They feel like **“No, absolutely not.”** They trigger disgust, fear, or a sense of betrayal. This chapter is for those moments.

It is not written as a conversion lever. It is written as a translator: **outside “ick” → inside “what it protects.”** You can still disagree. The goal is that your disagreement becomes *accurate* rather than *reactive*.

## A caution before we touch the flashpoints

If you use a stumbling block as a weapon, you will make it harder to love truth and neighbor at the same time.

- **If you are inside a tradition**: don’t use this chapter to prove you’re “normal” and everyone else is “weird.”
- **If you are outside a tradition**: don’t use this chapter as a checklist of reasons to despise your neighbor.

The purpose is modest: to let you say, “I see what you are trying to protect,” even if you still say, “I don’t agree that your method is right.”

And if any of these topics reliably spikes your anxiety or anger, you’re allowed to skim or skip. The goal is de‑escalation, not self‑harm.

If you want a simple way to use this chapter:

1. Read the **outside “ick”** as a real moral/psychological signal, not as a punchline.
2. Read the **inside “protection”** as a best‑case explanation, not a demand.
3. Name the **good being protected** on both sides, and only then decide what you think is true.

## 1) Mary and “Theotokos” (outside: “Mary worship”)

### The outside “ick”

For many Protestants (and some Catholics), Marian language can feel like a rival gospel: prayer directed toward Mary, titles that feel too exalted, and devotional intensity that looks like it displaces Christ.

It’s worth saying explicitly: this reaction is often trying to protect something good—**Christ’s uniqueness**, and prayer that remains clearly directed to God.

### The inside “protection”

At the level of *early* doctrinal conflict, “Theotokos” (God-bearer / Mother of God) was not meant as a sentimental elevation of Mary. It was meant as a **Christological guardrail**: the one Mary bore and birthed is truly God in the flesh, not a merely human figure later “inhabited” by divinity. Put bluntly: it’s a way of refusing to split Jesus into two subjects.

You can disagree about later Marian developments. But the original instinct is: **protect the confession that the Incarnation is real and personal**—God truly became man, in one Christ.[^1]

### A gentler way to argue, if you must

- Instead of: “You worship Mary.”
- Try: “I see the Christ-protecting intent of Theotokos. My concern is what later devotional practice *can* communicate or become.”

That turns a caricature into a tractable disagreement.

## 2) Icons and relics (outside: “idolatry”)

### The outside “ick”

If you were taught “images = idolatry,” then icon veneration can feel like a relapse into paganism: kissing images, candles, bowing, and language that seems to blur the Creator/creature line.

This fear is not automatically contempt. It often tries to protect something real: **the Creator/creature distinction**, and the moral seriousness of idolatry warnings.

### The inside “protection”

The icon instinct is not “God can be contained in matter.” It is closer to: **because God truly took on matter (a real face, a real body), matter can now bear witness without being a rival to God.**

The classic move is a distinction between **worship** (owed to God) and **honor/veneration** (given to persons and to representations of persons). Whatever you think about where that line should be drawn, the *thing being protected* is the Incarnation: Christianity refuses to treat the physical as a spiritual embarrassment.[^2]

### A gentler way to argue, if you must

- Instead of: “You’re bowing to wood and paint.”
- Try: “I see the Incarnation logic. My concern is how easily a practice meant as honor can be experienced as worship, especially without careful catechesis.”

That argues about *formation risks* rather than mocking.

## 3) The Eucharist (outside: “superstition” / “cannibalism” / “magic”)

### The outside “ick”

To many modern people, “real presence” language sounds like primitive magic. To some Protestants, it sounds like the gospel being replaced by ritual. To many Catholics and Orthodox, reducing the Eucharist to “a symbol” can feel like the most intimate thing in Christianity being treated as a mere reminder.

Underneath that protest is often a protection instinct too: a refusal to turn worship into **magical thinking**, and a refusal to let rituals become a substitute for repentance and faith.

### The inside “protection”

At its best, the Eucharist instinct protects a simple, startling claim: **God does not only forgive from a distance; God gives himself to be received.**

Early Christian texts already speak of the Eucharist in strong “reality” language (whatever later debates do with the metaphysics). That doesn’t settle later controversies, but it does explain why many Christians treat the Eucharist as a central *site of communion*, not a peripheral add‑on.[^3]

### A Reformation-era anchor (to reduce “nobody believed this until later”)

Even within the Reformation, Eucharistic belief was not monolithic. Lutheran confessional documents, for example, assert a strong presence claim in the Supper.[^4]

That doesn’t decide which account is correct. It does prevent a simplistic story where “historic Christianity” is all on one side and “serious Christians” are all on the other.

## 4) The papacy (outside: “centralization” / “infallibility”)

### The outside “ick”

For Protestants, papal claims can feel like a human office swallowing Scripture and conscience. For Orthodox Christians, they can feel like unilateral monarchy replacing conciliar life. Even many Catholics feel tension when papal authority is framed as if it were a replacement for the whole Church.

Underneath these reactions is a protective desire that deserves respect: **to limit spiritual power**, to protect conscience, and to keep “unity” from becoming coercion.

### The inside “protection”

The best version of the papal instinct is not “one man should be able to win arguments.” It is: **unity needs a visible center, and the Church needs a way to end disputes that otherwise fracture communion indefinitely.**

And when Catholics speak of infallibility, they often mean a narrowly defined claim under specific conditions (not “the pope is always right”), which is part of why Catholics can experience it as a protective boundary rather than a power grab.[^5]

The obvious counter‑question is whether centralized adjudication prevents fracture, or simply relocates fracture into protest. But the protection-logic is not trivial: it is the fear that without a final court, “the Church” becomes only a swarm of opinions.

## 5) “Works,” theosis, and transformation (outside: “earning salvation”)

### The outside “ick”

If you grew up hearing “it’s not by works,” any talk of ascetic effort, cooperation, merit, or transformation can feel like spiritual accounting: a ladder you climb, a wage you earn, a performance you must maintain.

This allergy often protects something precious: **grace as gift**, and the safety of not needing to earn God’s love.

### The inside “protection”

The inside concern is usually not “humans save themselves.” It is: **salvation heals.** A gospel that is only a verdict and never a transformation can feel psychologically thin and morally unstable. Theosis language (in Eastern Christianity) is an attempt to say, with maximal intensity, that grace is not only pardon but participation—God makes the human person truly alive.

Disagreements here are often about sequencing and meaning: what “faith” is, what “works” are, what grace does to the person, and what role sacraments play in that healing. Translating those terms before you fight is already most of the battle.

## 6) “Sola scriptura” (outside: “paper pope” / “private interpretation chaos”)

### The outside “ick”

From Catholic/Orthodox perspectives, “Scripture alone” can sound like declaring every individual the final authority—inviting fragmentation and doctrinal drift.

This concern is often trying to protect something communal: a Church that is more than a set of private interpretations, with some real **mechanism for shared teaching**.

### The inside “protection”

The inside concern is often pastoral and moral: **institutions can lie, and Scripture is a court of appeal.** Many Protestants are not trying to abolish tradition; they are trying to refuse coercion and corruption by insisting that the Church is accountable to something it did not create.

The disagreement then becomes clearer: *what* counts as faithful interpretation, *who* has authority to bind conscience, and *how* disputes are adjudicated without either tyranny or chaos.

## 7) Tongues and charismatic practices (outside: “weird” / “forced”)

### The outside “ick”

If you’ve been around manipulative altar calls, pressured manifestations, or environments where dissent is shamed, “tongues” can feel like social coercion dressed as spirituality.

That refusal can be a virtue: it protects **consent**, honesty, and the right to say “no” without being spiritually punished.

### The inside “protection”

For many charismatics, tongues can function as **surrender**: prayer that is not a performance of intelligence, and worship that breaks the need to control. In healthier contexts it is paired with discernment, consent, and humility; in unhealthy contexts it becomes a status marker and a tool of pressure.

This is one of the places where “what it protects” (surrender, expectancy) is real, and “how it fails” (coercion, volatility) is also real.

## A closing principle: ask “what wound is this protecting?”

Many stumbling blocks are attempts to protect against a real wound:

- **Mary / Theotokos**: protect the unity of Christ.
- **Icons**: protect the Incarnation against spiritualizing.
- **Eucharist**: protect communion from becoming mere ideas.
- **Papacy**: protect unity from endless fracture.
- **Theosis / transformation**: protect salvation from becoming mere verdict.
- **Sola scriptura**: protect conscience from institutional overreach.
- **Tongues**: protect prayer from becoming only cerebral control.

You can still judge that a protection mechanism is risky or wrong. But when you can name what it’s trying to protect, you stop needing contempt in order to disagree.
