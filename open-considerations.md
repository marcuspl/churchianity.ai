## 1. The Disproportionate Weight of Protestantism

**The Anomaly:** 
Because we correctly sub-divided the Protestant section (3.4.3) into three distinct streams (High/Confessional/Spirit-led) to maintain accuracy, that specific section is now structurally much larger and denser than the Orthodox and Catholic sections. 

**Editorial Decision:**
We will *keep* this disproportionate sizing rather than artificially pruning it for visual symmetry. 

**The Reasoning:**
1. **Historical/Philosophical correctness supersedes visual symmetry.** It is more important to accurately map the reality of these traditions than to force them into equal page counts.
2. **The structure is the message.** The fact that the Protestant section requires so much more space just to map its sub-divisions is itself an indicator of the structural reality of the tradition. It reflects the inherent, continuous splintering caused by hyper-individualism and *sola scriptura* without a shared magisterial umpire.
3. **Transparency:** We will add an explicit editorial note to the text acknowledging this disproportionate sizing. We will explain to the reader that the sprawling nature of that section is not an editorial bias favoring Protestantism, but rather an honest reflection of the historical and philosophical complexity that followed the Reformation.

*Status: Decision finalized. Agents do not need to trim their Protestant sections.*

**New Atheism → The Apologetic Trap (pulled from Ch 7)**

The "New Atheism" movement — Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris, Dennett, and their successors — challenged Christianity in explicitly empiricist terms: show us the evidence, submit your claims to falsification, defend your propositions or abandon them. The challenge was not new in substance — Hume and Russell had made similar arguments — but it was new in cultural reach, arriving through bestsellers, viral debates, and social media in a form that reached millions of laypeople who had never encountered Enlightenment-era philosophy of religion. Many Christian communities responded by accepting the terms of the debate: marshaling evidence, constructing logical proofs, treating faith as a hypothesis to be defended in the same register as a scientific claim. The apologetic industry that emerged — podcasts, debate circuits, evidence-based defenses of the Resurrection — was often intellectually serious and pastorally motivated. But the structural cost was subtle. By defending the faith in purely empiricist terms, the apophatic dimension was quietly abandoned. Mystery became a liability rather than a feature. The God who exceeds all categories was replaced, in public discourse, by a God who could be argued into plausibility using the same tools the critics employed. The traditions that had preserved the apophatic most carefully — Orthodox hesychasm, Catholic mysticism, Quaker silence — were largely absent from this exchange, not because they had nothing to say but because what they had to say could not be said in the register the debate demanded. The result, across much of Western Christianity, was a subtle flattening: a faith that could win arguments but had lost the vocabulary for mystery.
