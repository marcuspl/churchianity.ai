## 3.1–3.2 (labels + posture)

- **Clarify**: Gemini uses bracketed section headers like `**[Historically attested]**` rather than inline labels. That can work, but in the merge it may be worth standardizing to the shared convention (inline where it matters) so readers don’t mistake broad generalizations as “hard fact.”
- **Soften**: `3.2.A.4` uses “inevitably” in a question (“inevitably alter…”). Small word, but it primes fate-talk. Consider “tended to / often / plausibly.”

## 3.3 Reformation framing (Trent/Westphalia)

- **Soften**: `3.3.B.4` says Trent “condemned Protestant errors.” True in a sense, but the phrase can read like a partisan aside. Consider “condemned key Protestant positions” or “issued anathemas on disputed points” to keep a descriptive voice.
- **Clarify**: `3.3.B.5` links Westphalia (1648) with *cuius regio, eius religio*. That formula is more directly Augsburg (1555); Westphalia entrenches confessional political reality but is more complex. This is a facts nuance, but it also affects credibility/tone (readers will feel “you’re simplifying my history”).

## 3.4.3 Protestant risks (absolutes)

- **Soften**: `3.4.3.R1` “inevitably produces thousands” is rhetorically effective but too absolute. Suggest: “tends to produce proliferation” / “often produces multiplication” and, if needed, a short “unless stabilized by confessions/bishops/associations” clause.
- **Keep**: The `3.4.3` note acknowledging Protestant vastness is a good fairness move.

## 3.5.B “American friction point” (culture note)

- **Keep**: This is a high-signal analytic insight.
- **Soften**: The “oppression” language (`3.5.B.3`) can trigger defensiveness in readers who associate liturgical surrender with abuse histories. Suggest: keep the contrast but use slightly less loaded pairing (“freedom” vs “constraint/burden”) unless the text explicitly names abuse as a distinct issue.

## 4.2 Embodied practices (genre shift + neuroscience voice)

- **Keep**: `4.2.B` Incarnation framing is strong and matches the editor’s directive.
- **Soften**: `4.2.4` “physiologically alters psychological states” is plausible but reads like confident pop-neuroscience. Consider “often affects felt state / can shift attention and affect.”
- **Soften**: `4.2.6` “bypasses the language centers of the brain” is too strong as stated; better: “reduces ordinary language-production control” or “is associated with altered activity in language/control networks.” The added “deeply therapeutic” phrasing risks pushing the section into therapy-genre rather than Christian formation.
- **Clarify/Symmetry**: Ensure the “avoid extremes” rule is applied consistently: don’t let “neuro” language become the book’s authority voice.

## 4.4 Relief language

- **Keep**: `4.4.2` “Curiosity is not apostasy” is direct and likely relieving for the target audience.
- **Soften**: `4.4.4` “kings, emperors, and popes fighting over land…” is a valuable liberation move, but it’s easy to over-reduce. Suggest a small qualifier: “often also involved” / “was frequently entangled with” to keep historical modesty and avoid “it was all politics” vibes.
