## 3.2 East–West fracture (tone + determinism)

- **Keep**: `3.2.B.4` (1204) is handled with moral seriousness and avoids cheap polemic; `3.2.B.7` (1965 lifting) is exactly the sort of “laity don’t know this” relief moment the book needs.
- **Soften**: `3.2.B.3` uses “almost inevitable.” Determinism language can trigger readers who want to preserve contingency/responsibility. Suggest: “made later rupture far more likely / structurally pressured.”
- **Soften**: `3.2.C.1` ends with “better positioned than most scholars.” This reads as a needless flex; it could alienate academics and distract lay readers. Suggest: “better positioned than most lay accounts” or drop the comparison.
- **Clarify**: `3.2 meta-level` point (3) “post-hoc theological rationalization” risks sounding cynical (“theology as cover story”). Consider rephrasing as: “post-hoc narrative hardening” or “later theological framing can make the split feel more principled/inevitable than it felt at the time,” preserving the insight without implying bad faith.

## 3.2.A.3 conciliar stagnation (historical precision / labeling)

- **Clarify**: “Orthodox have not held an ecumenical council since 787” is commonly said but not universally agreed (depending on how “ecumenical” is defined and how later councils are counted in different traditions). Suggest adding a **Contested** label or a short qualifier (“in the common Orthodox enumeration…”).
- **Keep**: The 2016 Crete example is strong and concrete, but ensure it’s framed as illustrative rather than dispositive.

## 3.3 Reformation (tone + “inevitable”)

- **Keep**: `3.3.D.1` is a model of Pass 4 tone discipline: it honors Protestant/Catholic/Orthodox lived interpretations without collapsing into relativism.
- **Soften**: “structurally inevitable” appears in the meta-level argument. Same point can be made with less fate-talk: “structurally primed / under strong structural pressure / conditions made rupture durable.”
- **Clarify**: “succeeded not because theology was better” could be read as dismissive by Protestants. Suggest one sentence that grants theological conviction as a real driver while still emphasizing technology/politics as enabling conditions.

## 3.4.3 Protestant strengths/risks (avoid absolutes; preserve symmetry)

- **Soften**: `3.4.3.S1` “lower barriers to entry than any other tradition” is an absolute. Consider “often lowers barriers” / “tends to lower barriers” and name the relevant dimension (institutional access, vernacular, etc.).
- **Soften**: `3.4.3.R2` “every generation re-reads Scripture without the guardrail of received tradition” is too sweeping; many Protestant streams do have tradition/creeds/confessions functioning as guardrails. Suggest narrowing to “in many streams” or tying to particular sub-streams.
- **Keep/Symmetry**: The explicit pacing note about Protestant sub-streams dominating is exactly right. It should travel into the merge as an editorial constraint (or as an explicit “why this section is bigger” note, per editor decision).

## 3.6 protection systems (naming scandals without weaponizing)

- **Keep**: The `3.6.B.2` Pass 4 tone note is excellent: it pre-empts “you’re singling out Catholics” and explains why the example is structurally relevant.
- **Clarify/Symmetry**: Consider making the parallel non-Catholic examples *slightly* more visible in the immediate vicinity (even one clause), so the reader feels symmetry emotionally, not only in footnote-like references.

## 4.2 embodied practices (genre + neuroscience language)

- **Keep**: `4.2.B` Incarnation-as-genre-permission framing is the right bridge and should be preserved.
- **Soften**: “neuroscience confirms” (vagal tone / parasympathetic dominance) can sound overconfident. Consider phrasing as “suggests / is consistent with / studies indicate,” to keep humility and avoid “science proves prayer.”
- **Clarify**: `4.2.2` breath prayer compared to pranayama: helpful for readers, but it can trigger “syncretism” alarms. Consider adding one sentence explicitly distinguishing *mechanism similarity* from *theological meaning* (you already gesture at this; making it explicit would reduce fear).
- **Soften**: `4.2.5` “misreads the text” (Matt 6:7) is a bit sharp. Consider: “is often read as…” / “may not be the best reading…” to keep the reader from feeling corrected.
- **Clarify**: `4.2.6` “centuries-long dormancy” of glossolalia is contested (there are intermittent reports). Consider softening to “less visible in mainstream practice” rather than “dormant.”
